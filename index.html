SDG 2: Zero Hunger

End hunger, achieve food security and improved nutrition and promote sustainable agriculture

The Inception and Evolution of SDG 2

Sustainable Development Goal 2 (SDG 2) emerged from the United Nations' recognition of the fundamental right to adequate food and nutrition for all human beings. Established in 2015 as part of the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development, SDG 2 builds upon the foundation laid by the Millennium Development Goals (MDGs) while expanding its scope to address the complex interconnections between hunger, food security, nutrition, and sustainable agriculture.

The inception of SDG 2 was driven by the alarming reality that despite global progress in reducing hunger, approximately 690 million people still go to bed hungry each night. This goal represents a comprehensive approach to ending hunger, acknowledging that the challenge extends beyond simply producing more food – it encompasses issues of access, sustainability, and nutritional quality.

Comprehensive Challenges in Achieving Zero Hunger

Environmental Challenges

Climate change poses an unprecedented threat to food security worldwide. Rising temperatures, changing precipitation patterns, and increasing frequency of extreme weather events directly impact agricultural productivity. Soil degradation and biodiversity loss further compound these challenges, threatening the sustainability of food production systems.

Socioeconomic Barriers

Poverty remains inextricably linked to hunger, creating a vicious cycle that's difficult to break. Income inequality limits access to nutritious food, while lack of education and resources prevents small-scale farmers from adopting more efficient agricultural practices. Gender inequality particularly affects food security, as women farmers often face additional barriers to resources and markets.

Structural Issues

Food waste and loss in supply chains significantly impact food availability. Inefficient distribution systems, inadequate storage facilities, and market access barriers contribute to this problem. Additionally, political instability and conflicts in various regions disrupt food production and distribution networks.
